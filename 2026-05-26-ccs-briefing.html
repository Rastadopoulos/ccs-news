Canada presses oil sands producers on Pathways carbon capture project

World Oil  /  Bloomberg · 25 May 2026 · [North America] · Bloomberg version [paywall]

Canadian Energy Minister Tim Hodgson said he is “highly confident” that Alberta oil sands companies can absorb the cost of the C$16.5 billion (~US$12 billion) Pathways Alliance CCS project — a proposed 400+ km CO₂ pipeline network connecting capture plants north of Edmonton to an underground injection hub in eastern Alberta, designed to store up to 16 Mtpa by 2035. The minister’s confidence follows a new federal-provincial agreement on Alberta’s industrial carbon price trajectory. The Oil Sands Alliance (Cenovus Energy, Imperial Oil, Suncor Energy, ConocoPhillips Canada, and MEG Energy) has publicly stated the deal “still maintains uncompetitive costs.” A binding tripartite financing agreement between producers, the Province of Alberta, and Ottawa remains unresolved after a missed April 1 deadline; Alberta is expected to unveil its proposed pipeline route by July 1 as the next regulatory step.

COFs redefine membrane limits in carbon capture

Mirage News · 25 May 2026 · [International] · via Shelly Murrell weekly monitoring

Researchers at Tohoku University (Japan) developed two new heteroatom-engineered covalent organic framework (COF) porous materials — TUS-621 (oxygen-rich) and TUS-622 (sulfur-bearing) — incorporated into mixed-matrix membranes (MMMs) for CO₂ gas separation. The oxygen-rich TUS-621 demonstrated stronger affinity for CO₂ molecules and superior transport performance, enabling both permeability and selectivity to exceed the 2008 Robeson upper bound — a longstanding engineering benchmark for polymer membranes. The study, published in the Journal of the American Chemical Society (21 May 2026), suggests a pathway to more energy-efficient membrane-based CO₂ separation relevant to post-combustion capture and direct air capture pre-concentration stages.

Moomba CCS project achieves historic 2 million tonne CO₂e milestone

Santos · 20 May 2026 · [Australia, Projects] · via Shelly Murrell weekly monitoring

Santos and partner Beach Energy announced that Australia’s Moomba CCS project (Cooper Basin, SA) has permanently stored 2 million tonnes of CO₂-equivalent in just over 18 months of operation — equivalent to removing approximately 826,000 cars from South Australian roads — despite two significant flooding events during the period. The project has generated more than 1.19 million ACCUs to date. Santos noted the Cooper/Eromanga Basin’s potential to support up to 20 Mtpa injection for 50 years, positioning Moomba as the foundation for a commercial third-party carbon storage business targeting hard-to-abate industrial emitters.

Eni and BlackRock’s GIP score over $670 million financing boost to fuel CCS growth

Offshore Energy · 22 May 2026 · [Europe, Markets] · via Shelly Murrell weekly monitoring

Eni CCUS Holding — the strategic JV between Eni and BlackRock’s Global Infrastructure Partners (GIP) — secured a £500 million (~$670 million) project-finance facility from a syndicate of 13 international lenders including BNP Paribas, ING, MUFG, NatWest and Societe Generale, with demand significantly exceeding the initial target amount. The facility builds on the financial close of the Liverpool Bay CCS project (the HyNet backbone, 4.5 Mtpa phase-1 capacity, targeting 2028 operations) and will support Eni’s Dutch L10-CCS project and the proposed Bacton CCS hub (UK), with the Ravenna CCS project in Italy also in the portfolio.

Industry groups call for phased EU approach to CO₂ transport regulation

Carbon Herald · 23 May 2026 · [Europe, Policy] · via Shelly Murrell weekly monitoring

A coalition of 21 industry stakeholders — spanning project operators, infrastructure developers, and trade associations — published a joint statement calling on the European Commission to implement a flexible, phased regulatory framework for CO₂ transport under the forthcoming Carbon Management Infrastructure regulation. The statement warns that premature tariff methodologies or rigid third-party access rules could undermine investor confidence and damage first-mover projects. The groups advocated for support for cross-border regional CO₂ transport corridors, negotiated commercial agreements during early deployment phases, and a prohibition on retroactive policy changes affecting already-bankable projects.
