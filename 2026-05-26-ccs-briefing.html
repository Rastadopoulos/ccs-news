Canada backs Pathways Alliance CCS; Energy Minister presses oil sands producers to commit

Bloomberg  /  World Oil · 25 May 2026 · [North America]

Canadian Energy Minister Tim Hodgson said he is “highly confident” Alberta oil sands companies can absorb the cost of the proposed Pathways CCS megaproject. The Pathways Alliance — comprising Cenovus, Canadian Natural, ConocoPhillips, Imperial Oil, and Suncor — is developing a 400+ km CO₂ pipeline network connecting multiple oilsands capture plants north of Edmonton to an underground injection hub in the Cold Lake area of eastern Alberta. The headline target is 16 million tonnes per annum (Mtpa) stored by 2035, though the Ottawa-Alberta implementation agreement sets a binding floor of 6 Mtpa in-service by that date. Estimated capital cost is C$16.5 billion (~US$12 billion). The three-way cost-and-risk split among producers, the Province of Alberta, and the federal government remains unresolved after an April 2026 deadline passed; PM Mark Carney’s government recently reached agreement with Alberta on the industrial carbon price trajectory. Minister Hodgson expects a financing deal to be finalised later in 2026.

Pathways Alliance CCS: Ottawa-Alberta deal formalises 6 Mtpa minimum, original 16 Mtpa ambition scaled back

National Observer · 20 May 2026 · [North America]

Reporting on the implementation text of the Ottawa-Alberta oilsands agreement reveals it commits to a minimum 6 Mtpa CCS capacity in-service by 2035 — well below the Pathways Alliance’s headline 16 Mtpa figure. The change reflects negotiators’ preference for a credible near-term floor over an aspirational ceiling. The five major oil sands producers have recently signalled reduced enthusiasm for the project, and the three-way financing structure between industry, province, and federal government is yet to be agreed. The Pathways project, if fully built, would be the world’s largest CCS development.

Greensand Future: EU’s first commercial offshore CO₂ storage project approaching mid-2026 operations

Greensand Future / INEOS · Ongoing 2026 reporting · [Europe — Denmark]

The INEOS-led Greensand Future project — the first full-scale offshore CO₂ storage facility in the European Union — is targeting injection start in mid-2026 at the Nini West field, approximately 240 km from Esbjerg. Initial storage capacity is 400,000 tonnes/year, with ambitions to scale to 4–8 Mtpa by 2030. The dedicated liquefied-CO₂ carrier Carbon Destroyer 1 arrived at Port Esbjerg in late March 2026 ahead of first operations; the onshore CO₂ transit terminal has been operational since 2025. Partners are INEOS E&P, Harbour Energy, and Nordsøfonden.
